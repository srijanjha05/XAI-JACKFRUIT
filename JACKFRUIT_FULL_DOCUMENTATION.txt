SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM — WHY THIS EXISTS
================================================================================

1.1  The Clinical Crisis
------------------------
Alzheimer's Disease (AD) affects over 55 million people worldwide and is
expected to triple by 2050. It is a progressive, irreversible neurodegenera-
tive disease causing the death of neurons and collapse of neural connectivity,
ultimately stripping a person of memory, language, and executive function.

The disease begins silently — sometimes decades before clinical symptoms
appear. By the time a patient is diagnosed, catastrophic, irreversible damage
has already occurred. Early MRI-based detection is therefore critical: every
year of earlier detection is a year gained for intervention.

1.2  The Black Box Problem
--------------------------
Deep learning models — particularly 3D CNNs — can classify brain MRI scans
with high accuracy. But they operate as "black boxes." A CNN that outputs
"this patient has Alzheimer's" gives no explanation to a clinician. The
critical questions left unanswered are:

  - Is the AI using a real, clinically recognized biomarker?
  - Is it detecting cortical atrophy, or an imaging artifact?
  - Is the diagnosis trustworthy for this specific patient?

Without these answers, medical AI cannot be adopted in clinical settings
regardless of accuracy. This is the fundamental motivation for Explainable
Artificial Intelligence (XAI).

1.3  This Project's Goals
--------------------------
XAI-JACKFRUIT has three interlocked goals:

  GOAL 1: Build a high-accuracy 3D CNN classifier for AD vs. CN on OASIS-1.
  GOAL 2: Use XAI techniques (LRP + IG) to make the CNN's reasoning visible
          and quantifiable per tissue region and per patient.
  GOAL 3: Enrich the explanation space with novel spatial, lateralization,
          and inter-method features, generating publishable per-patient
          clinical reports.

SECTION 2: THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS
================================================================================

2.1  3D DenseNet Architecture
-------------------------------
The backbone CNN is a 3D DenseNet (Densely Connected Convolutional Network,
Huang et al. 2017). In standard CNNs, each layer receives input only from the
previous layer. DenseNet connects every layer to all subsequent layers via
skip connections, creating a dense connectivity pattern:

    x_l = H_l([x_0, x_1, ..., x_{l-1}])

This design has two critical advantages for 3D medical imaging:

  a) Feature reuse: early spatial features (edges, sulcal geometry) are
     directly accessible to deep layers, so the network does not need to
     re-learn them.
  b) Gradient flow: the dense connections prevent vanishing gradients, which
     are a severe problem in 3D volumetric convolution over small datasets.

The architecture uses dense blocks with growth rate k, interleaved with
compression transition layers, ending in a multimodal fusion head that
combines the 3D CNN feature vector with a 6-dimensional tabular feature
vector (Age, Sex, eTIV, nWBV, MMSE, MMSE_valid).

2.2  Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP)
--------------------------------------------
LRP (Bach et al. 2015) is a backward-pass attribution method. Given a trained
network and its output score f(x) for a specific class, LRP decomposes that
score back through the network until each input voxel receives a "relevance"
score R_i satisfying the conservation law:

    f(x) = sum_i R_i

This is unlike gradient-based methods, which measure local sensitivity. LRP
measures how much each input contributed — not just how sensitive the output
is to small perturbations at that point.

The specific variant used here is LRP-CMPalpha1beta0 (Composite Alpha-1
Beta-0), which applies the alpha-beta rule to convolutional layers:

    R_j = sum_k [ (a_j * w_jk^+) / (sum_{j'} a_{j'} * w_{j'k}^+) ] * R_k
           - sum_k [ (a_j * w_jk^-) / (sum_{j'} a_{j'} * w_{j'k}^-) ] * R_k

With alpha=1, beta=0, only positive weights contribute, producing a non-
negative relevance map that is physically interpretable: positive relevance =
this voxel is evidence FOR the predicted class (AD); negative relevance
= this voxel is evidence AGAINST the predicted class.

2.3  Integrated Gradients (IG)
--------------------------------
IG (Sundararajan et al. 2017) is an axiomatic attribution method based on
integrating the gradient of the output with respect to the input along a
straight path from a baseline (zero image) to the actual input:

    IG_i(x) = (x_i - x_i') * integral_0^1 [df(x' + alpha*(x-x'))/dx_i] dalpha

IG satisfies two key axioms:
  - Sensitivity: if a feature changes the output and the baseline doesn't
    have it, it gets non-zero attribution.
  - Implementation invariance: two networks computing the same function
    get the same attribution.

JACKFRUIT uses LRP as the primary attribution and IG as the secondary
attribution to compute inter-method agreement (see Section 4.4).

2.4  W-Score Normalization
---------------------------
Raw LRP relevance values are confounded by brain size (eTIV), age, and sex.
W-score normalization (used in Singh et al. 2025 and retained here) uses
linear regression on the CN cohort to build a normative model:

    feature_predicted = beta_0 + beta_1*Age + beta_2*Sex + beta_3*eTIV

Then for any subject:
    W = (feature_observed - feature_predicted) / SD_residuals

W = 0 means the subject's value is exactly at the CN norm.
|W| > 2: clinically notable deviation (>2 SD from norm)
|W| > 3: strong pathological signal
|W| > 4: severe pathology

W-scores allow direct cross-subject comparison of XAI features regardless
of demographic confounders.

2.5  Unsupervised Clustering for Biological Validation
-------------------------------------------------------
After W-scoring, features are fed into Agglomerative Ward Clustering.
This is a bottom-up hierarchical clustering that minimizes within-cluster
variance (Ward linkage):

    Delta(A, B) = |A|*|B| / (|A|+|B|) * ||centroid_A - centroid_B||^2

Clustering is intentionally unsupervised — it does not know the AD/CN labels.
If the clusters align with clinical diagnoses, it validates that the XAI
features carry real biological signal. Metrics used:

  - Homogeneity: purity of clusters w.r.t. true labels (are all members of
    a cluster the same class?)
  - Completeness: are all members of a true class in the same cluster?
  - V-measure: harmonic mean of H and C (overall agreement)
  - ARI: agreement corrected for chance
  - Silhouette: geometric compactness vs. separation
  - Davies-Bouldin: ratio of intra-cluster scatter to inter-cluster distance

In Singh et al. (2025), V-measure = 0.43 on 3253 subjects across 6 cohorts.
JACKFRUIT achieves V-measure = 0.234 on 436 OASIS-1 subjects — lower, but
scientifically appropriate given the much smaller dataset and the known
clinical heterogeneity of AD on OASIS-1.

SECTION 3: THE DATASET — OASIS-1
================================================================================

3.1  What is OASIS-1?
----------------------
OASIS-1 (Open Access Series of Imaging Studies, Cross-Sectional Release 1)
is a publicly available neuroimaging dataset from Washington University,
St. Louis. It contains structural MRI scans from 436 subjects aged 18–96.

Key properties:
  - 336 subjects labeled CN (CDR = 0)
  - 100 subjects labeled AD (CDR > 0: 70 with CDR=0.5, 28 with CDR=1,
    2 with CDR=2)
  - Binarized label: CDR=0 → CN, CDR>0 → AD
  - MRI registered to MNI152 T88 space, voxel dimension (176, 208, 176)
  - FSL tissue segmentation included (0=background, 1=CSF, 2=GM, 3=WM)
  - Key clinical biomarker: nWBV (AD mean=0.722 vs CN mean=0.812)

3.2  Class Imbalance
---------------------
OASIS-1 has a 3.36:1 CN:AD ratio (336:100). This is a significant imbalance
that, if unaddressed, causes the model to learn CN-biased predictions. A
naive model that always predicts CN achieves 77.1% accuracy — misleadingly
high. All JACKFRUIT training improvements were designed to counteract this.

3.3  MMSE Data Corruption — A Critical Bug Fixed in JACKFRUIT
--------------------------------------------------------------
The OASIS-1 CSV file contains an MMSE column, but 201 of the 436 subjects
have corrupted entries. These are predominantly young, healthy CN subjects
who had no cognitive examination — their MMSE column was inadvertently
populated with eTIV values (large numbers like 1200–1500), which are
completely outside the valid MMSE range of 0–30.

All 100 AD subjects have valid MMSE scores (clinically assessed).

XAI-ORANGE (the baseline) used this column raw, introducing numerical
noise into tabular features. JACKFRUIT fixes this with:

  a) Robust regex parsing — extracts the first numeric value within [0,30]
  b) Binary MMSE_valid flag — set to 1 if the MMSE is valid, 0 otherwise

The MMSE_valid flag is itself clinically informative: a 0 flag strongly
correlates with CN status (no young AD patient exists in OASIS-1). This
transforms a data-quality flaw into a usable feature.

SECTION 4: NOVEL CONTRIBUTIONS (JACKFRUIT EXTENSIONS)
================================================================================

JACKFRUIT introduces five novel XAI contributions beyond the baseline
framework (XAI-ORANGE and Singh et al. 2025). Each is described below with
its theoretical justification, implementation, and clinical significance.

4.1  Spatial Moments of LRP Attribution Maps
---------------------------------------------
ORANGE and Singh et al. (2025) extract only three quantities per tissue
region: total positive relevance, total negative relevance, and density.
These are scalars that summarize "how much" the CNN looked at each tissue,
but lose all information about the spatial distribution of that attention.

JACKFRUIT computes the full second-order and higher statistical moments of
the LRP relevance distribution within each FSL tissue mask:

  Mean        — average relevance magnitude; overall signal strength
  Std Dev     — spread of relevance values; uniform vs. variable attention
  Skewness    — asymmetry of distribution; dominant tail behavior
  Kurtosis    — excess kurtosis; detects hotspots (leptokurtic = peaked)
  Entropy     — Shannon entropy of relevance histogram; diffuse vs. focal

Shannon entropy is computed as:
    H = -sum_bins p_i * log2(p_i)    where p_i = bin_count / total_count

These 5 statistics × 3 tissues = 15 new spatial features per subject.

Clinical significance:
  - Focal atrophy (early hippocampal AD): high kurtosis, low entropy,
    high CR. The CNN's attention is concentrated in a small anatomical
    region.
  - Diffuse atrophy (posterior cortical AD, advanced stages): low kurtosis,
    high entropy. The CNN's attention is spread across the cortex.

This distinction is clinically important: two patients with the same total
LRP relevance in Gray Matter may have entirely different disease morphologies.
Spatial moments allow the clustering to detect AD subtypes, not just severity.

4.2  Hemisphere Asymmetry Index (HAI)
--------------------------------------
Alzheimer's Disease has a well-documented lateralization pattern in its
early stages: left-temporal predominance (particularly entorhinal cortex,
hippocampus, and parahippocampal gyrus). This asymmetry is clinically
measurable on MRI and has been reported in multiple neuropathological studies.

JACKFRUIT introduces the Hemisphere Asymmetry Index as a novel XAI-derived
biomarker — the first time (to our knowledge) the CNN's own attribution map
is used to quantify per-subject hemispheric lateralization:

    HAI = (R_right - R_left) / (R_right + R_left + epsilon)

where R_right = sum of positive LRP relevance in right hemisphere voxels
      R_left  = sum of positive LRP relevance in left hemisphere voxels
                within the tissue mask (GM, WM, or CSF)

The brain is split at the sagittal midline x=88 in MNI T88 space (voxel
coordinate, not mm).

Interpretation:
  HAI > 0  — right hemisphere dominance
  HAI < 0  — left hemisphere dominance
  |HAI| ≈ 0 — bilateral / symmetric

HAI is computed per tissue (CSF, GM, WM) and W-scored against the CN
normative distribution. In the OASIS-1 AD cohort, Gray Matter HAI shows
negative values (left-dominant) consistent with known neuroanatomy —
providing cross-validation of the CNN's internal reasoning against
established clinical literature without any anatomical supervision.

4.3  Concentration Ratio (CR)
-------------------------------
The Concentration Ratio measures the spatial sparsity of the CNN's attention
within each tissue region:

    CR = sum(top 10% largest |R_i|) / sum(all |R_i|)

CR ranges from 0 to 1:
  CR → 1.0: the CNN's relevance is concentrated in a small fraction of
            voxels — indicates a focal, localized pathological hotspot
  CR → 0.0: the CNN's relevance is broadly distributed — indicates diffuse,
            generalized degeneration

This separates the "where" from the "how much." Two subjects with identical
total LRP relevance in Gray Matter (same density score) may have:
  - High CR: one dense hippocampal hotspot (focal, early stage)
  - Low CR: diffuse parietal thinning (generalized, advanced stage)

ORANGE cannot distinguish these two scenarios. CR closes this gap and
enables the clustering to detect morphological subtypes.

4.4  Multi-XAI Inter-Method Agreement Score
--------------------------------------------
ORANGE uses only LRP for attribution. JACKFRUIT runs two fundamentally
different attribution methods on every subject:

  - LRP-CMPalpha1beta0: backward-pass, conservation-based
  - Integrated Gradients: path-integral of gradients, axiomatically grounded

For each subject and each tissue region, JACKFRUIT computes the Spearman
rank correlation between the voxel-level LRP and IG attribution maps:

    rho_tissue = Spearman(LRP_voxels within tissue, IG_voxels within tissue)

Spearman (not Pearson) is used because LRP and IG have different value scales
and distributions — rank correlation is invariant to monotonic rescaling.

Interpretation:
  rho >= 0.6:  high confidence — both methods agree on relevant voxels
  0.3 <= rho < 0.6:  moderate confidence
  rho < 0.3:  low confidence — methods disagree, explanation may be
              unreliable for this subject

Subjects with low agreement may represent:
  - Edge cases where the CNN is uncertain about the classification
  - Patients with atypical or non-standard atrophy morphology
  - Scans with imaging quality issues that affect attribution stability

This inter-method agreement score serves as a per-subject XAI reliability
indicator — a feature that does not exist in any prior framework applied to
OASIS-1, and which Singh et al. (2025) also lack (they use only LRP).

Clinically, this is the equivalent of asking: "Does the second expert
radiologist agree with the first?" A disagreement flags the case for
additional scrutiny.

4.5  Rule-Based Textual Explanation Reports
--------------------------------------------
JACKFRUIT generates a structured, human-readable clinical report for every
patient, synthesizing all W-scored XAI features into a narrative.

Each report contains:
  1. Patient ID, true diagnosis, CNN prediction, AD probability, threshold
  2. XAI reliability rating (derived from inter-method agreement score)
  3. Per-tissue pathological findings:
       - LRP relevance W-score with severity classification
         (mild: |W|=2–3, moderate: |W|=3–4, strong: |W|>4)
       - HAI lateralization (left-dominant, right-dominant, bilateral)
       - CR pattern (focal hotspot, diffuse spread)
       - Entropy pattern (concentrated, diffuse)
  4. Volumetric evidence (nWBV W-score vs. age/sex/eTIV CN norm)
  5. Low-agreement warning flag if rho < 0.3

This extends the rule-based reporting approach of Singh et al. (2025) with
lateralization, concentration, and multi-method confidence. It transforms
raw feature vectors into clinical decision-support language — a step toward
practical clinical deployment.

Example output snippet:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Patient ID: OAS1_0001_MR1 | True: AD | Predicted: AD
  AD Probability: 0.812  (threshold: 0.55)
  XAI Reliability: HIGH (LRP-IG Spearman rho = 0.681)

  Gray Matter:
    Relevance W-score: +3.41  [STRONG PATHOLOGY]
    CNN showed ELEVATED relevance in Gray Matter (+3.41 SD above CN norm)
    Lateralization: LEFT-hemisphere dominant  (HAI W = -2.18)
    Attribution: FOCAL concentrated hotspot  (CR W = +2.44)

  Volume: nWBV W-score = -3.72 [STRONG ATROPHY]
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: TRAINING IMPROVEMENTS OVER ORANGE
================================================================================

The XAI-ORANGE baseline achieved:
  AUC = 0.9146
  Accuracy = 81.67%

JACKFRUIT introduces six training improvements to address the root causes
of this sub-optimal performance.

5.1  Focal Loss (replacing Cross-Entropy)
------------------------------------------
Standard binary cross-entropy treats every training example equally. In an
imbalanced dataset (3.36:1 CN:AD), the model achieves a low loss by
correctly classifying the CN majority while making errors on AD. The loss
gradient is dominated by easy CN examples.

Focal Loss (Lin et al. 2017, originally for object detection) adds a
modulating factor that down-weights easy examples:

    FL(p_t) = -alpha * (1 - p_t)^gamma * log(p_t)

With gamma=2, alpha=0.25:
  - If p_t is high (easy correct prediction): (1-p_t)^2 ≈ 0 → near-zero loss
  - If p_t is low (hard, misclassified AD): (1-p_t)^2 ≈ 1 → full loss

This forces the model to invest its learning signal in the hard AD cases
that standard cross-entropy would effectively ignore after partial training.

5.2  Balanced Batch Sampling
------------------------------
Each training batch is explicitly constructed with exactly 50% CN and
50% AD subjects (sampled with replacement from the minority AD class).

This is conceptually distinct from class weighting: balanced batching
changes the gradient estimator at every step, ensuring the optimizer sees
equal evidence for both classes at every update. Combined with focal loss,
this creates a dual-correction of the imbalance: balanced exposure (batch
sampling) + gradient emphasis on hard examples (focal loss).

5.3  3D Augmentation
----------------------
During training, two augmentations are applied:

  a) Random Left-Right sagittal flip (probability 0.5):
     Biologically valid — the early-stage left-temporal dominance in AD
     is statistically weak at the individual level. The model must not
     overfit to hemisphere configuration in the training set.

  b) Gaussian noise injection (sigma ~ Uniform(0.01, 0.03), probability 0.3):
     Adds stochasticity to prevent overfitting to MRI scanner-specific
     intensity distributions (OASIS-1 is from a single scanner).

5.4  Test-Time Augmentation (TTA)
----------------------------------
At inference time, predictions are averaged over the original scan and
its LR-flipped counterpart:

    p_final = (p_original + p_flipped) / 2

This reduces prediction variance from spatial overfitting. The model's
response to the original and mirrored scan should be nearly symmetric;
averaging stabilizes the probability estimate and reduces false positives
caused by spatial artifacts in specific hemispheres.

5.5  F1-Optimized Decision Threshold
--------------------------------------
The classification threshold is not fixed at 0.5. After training, the
threshold is selected on the validation fold to maximize F1-score:

    F1 = 2 * precision * recall / (precision + recall)

In an imbalanced dataset, F1-optimization balances sensitivity and
precision without collapsing to one extreme. The optimal threshold found
in the JACKFRUIT run was 0.55 — slightly above 0.5, reflecting that the
calibrated posterior probability slightly underestimates AD risk.

ORANGE used the Youden-J criterion (max Sensitivity + Specificity) on the
test set rather than validation set, which is a form of test set leakage.

5.6  ReduceLROnPlateau Callback
---------------------------------
A learning rate scheduler monitors validation loss and reduces the learning
rate by a factor of 0.5 if it does not improve over a patience window of
5 epochs. Starting learning rate: 5e-5. This allows fine-grained convergence
after the initial rapid training phase without requiring manual intervention.

SECTION 6: THE PIPELINE — HOW EVERYTHING CONNECTS
================================================================================

The JACKFRUIT pipeline consists of 7 scripts executed sequentially:

  convert_to_nifti.py       — Convert raw OASIS .hdr/.img to .nii.gz
  oasis_0_train_cnn.py      — Train 3D DenseNet with all improvements
  oasis_1_extract_activations.py  — LRP + IG; spatial moments, HAI, CR
  oasis_1b_xai_agreement.py — Per-subject LRP-IG Spearman agreement
  oasis_2_wscore.py         — W-score normalization against CN baseline
  oasis_3_cluster_compare.py — 5-configuration clustering evaluation
  oasis_4_textual_report.py — Per-patient clinical text generation

Master runner: src/run_pipeline.sh

Step-by-step data flow:

  [RAW MRI SCANS]
       |
       v
  convert_to_nifti.py → .nii.gz files in data/nifti_converted/
       |
       v
  oasis_0_train_cnn.py → model .h5, ROC curve, per-subject probability CSV
                         in train/{EXP_NAME}/cv_{fold}/
       |
       v
  oasis_1_extract_activations.py → per-subject LRP and IG volumetric maps,
    extract tissue-masked statistics (moments, HAI, CR), saved as .xlsx
    in act_seg/{EXP_NAME}/cv_{fold}/{method}/
       |
  oasis_1b_xai_agreement.py → per-subject LRP-IG Spearman scores
    saved as xai_agreement_node{N}.csv
       |
       v
  oasis_2_wscore.py → W-scored feature CSVs
    in measures_combined/{EXP_NAME}/cv_{fold}/{method}/
       |
       v
  oasis_3_cluster_compare.py → clustering metrics across 5 configs
    in 4_clustering/ (CSVs + figures)
       |
       v
  oasis_4_textual_report.py → reports.txt + textual_reports.csv
    in reports/{EXP_NAME}/

================================================================================
SECTION 8: ORANGE → JACKFRUIT — WHAT CHANGED AND WHY
================================================================================

XAI-ORANGE was the first version of this pipeline, establishing the baseline
approach: train a 3D DenseNet, extract LRP maps, compute tissue relevance
sums, W-score them, and cluster. It demonstrated that the CNN is using real
tissue biomarkers — a meaningful result. But it had clear limitations:

8.1  What ORANGE Did
---------------------
  - 3D DenseNet with standard binary cross-entropy loss
  - Random batch sampling (no class balancing)
  - LRP only, no second XAI method
  - 3 features per tissue: pos_rel, neg_rel, density
  - W-scored features, Agglomerative clustering
  - No lateralization analysis
  - No spatial distribution analysis
  - No inter-method reliability measure
  - No patient-level textual reports
  - MMSE used raw (corrupted for ~201 subjects)

  ORANGE Result: AUC = 0.9146, Accuracy = 81.67%

8.2  What JACKFRUIT Changed
-----------------------------

A. TRAINING CHANGES:
   ─────────────────
   1. Focal Loss (gamma=2, alpha=0.25)
      Why: Standard cross-entropy was producing CN-biased predictions.
      The AD sensitivity was insufficient for clinical use. Focal loss
      forces learning signal onto hard AD cases.

   2. Balanced Batch Sampling
      Why: 3.36:1 imbalance was systematically under-representing AD in
      every gradient update. Balanced batching gives the optimizer equal
      evidence per class at every step.

   3. Test-Time Augmentation (LR flip averaging)
      Why: Single-pass predictions were unstable near the decision boundary.
      TTA averages bilateral predictions to reduce spatial overfitting.

   4. F1-Optimized Threshold (validation set)
      Why: ORANGE used Youden-J on the test set — introducing test set
      leakage. JACKFRUIT selects threshold on validation only.

   5. MMSE Data Repair + MMSE_valid binary flag
      Why: 201/436 subjects had corrupted MMSE entries (eTIV values stored
      in MMSE column). This introduced numerical noise. The repair + flag
      converts a data quality problem into a usable clinical feature.

   6. ReduceLROnPlateau
      Why: Fixed learning rate prevented fine-grained convergence in later
      epochs. Adaptive scheduling allows the optimizer to settle.

B. XAI CHANGES:
   ─────────────
   1. Spatial Moments (5 new statistics per tissue × 3 tissues = 15 features)
      Why: ORANGE knew "how much" but not "how." Moments reveal focal vs.
      diffuse atrophy morphology — critical for AD subtyping.

   2. Hemisphere Asymmetry Index (HAI)
      Why: AD has documented left-temporal predominance. ORANGE treated the
      brain as symmetric. HAI allows the CNN's attribution to reflect known
      neurological lateralization.

   3. Concentration Ratio (CR)
      Why: Two subjects with equal total LRP relevance may have completely
      different spatial patterns. CR distinguishes focal hotspot from
      diffuse degeneration.

   4. Integrated Gradients (second XAI method)
      Why: Using a single attribution method provides no reliability
      baseline. IG provides an independent, axiomatically-grounded check.

   5. Inter-Method Agreement Score (LRP-IG Spearman rho)
      Why: Per-subject reliability measure. Subjects where LRP and IG
      disagree are flagged as uncertain — clinically actionable information.

   6. Automated Textual Clinical Reports
      Why: Raw W-scores are not clinically interpretable by non-ML
      practitioners. Reports translate XAI numbers into clinical language.

8.3  What Stayed the Same
--------------------------
  - 3D DenseNet architecture (same growth rate, dense block structure)
  - Multimodal fusion (CNN features + tabular features)
  - FSL tissue segmentation masks (CSF, GM, WM)
  - W-score normalization (regression on CN baseline)
  - Agglomerative Ward Clustering
  - LRP-CMPalpha1beta0 as primary attribution method
  - OASIS-1 dataset, binarized CDR labels (CN vs. AD)

  - Singh et al.: V-measure = 0.43 (benefit of large N). JACKFRUIT:

framework from multi-cohort volumetric context enrichment to per-patient
XAI-intrinsic spatial enrichment, while also upgrading the classifier to

SECTION 10: HOW WE REACHED 87.5% ACCURACY — THE JOURNEY
================================================================================

Early Phase (March 2026 — ORANGE baseline):
  - Run 2026-03-24_0015: AUC=0.9146, Acc=81.67%
    This was the raw ORANGE result. Strong AUC (the ranking is correct)
    but moderate accuracy due to threshold issues and CN bias.

Middle Phase (March 2026 — early JACKFRUIT attempts):
  - Run 2026-03-25_2217, 2026-03-25_2236: AUC=0.62–0.69, Acc=57–77%
    These runs degraded performance. This was caused by partial
    implementation of balanced batching without focal loss — creating
    an over-aggressive correction that destabilized training. The loss
    curve was oscillating and not converging.

  - Diagnosis: balanced sampling alone, without focal loss to provide
    the correct gradient weighting, causes the model to oscillate between
    CN-biased and AD-biased predictions.

  - Fix: both balanced batching AND focal loss must be active simultaneously.
    Balanced batching = equal data exposure. Focal loss = correct gradient
    weighting. They are complementary, not redundant.

Final Phase (April 2026 — JACKFRUIT complete):
  - Run 2026-04-05_1904: AUC=0.925, Acc=86.36%
    First successful full JACKFRUIT training run. All improvements active.
    Accuracy already exceeds ORANGE by +4.7pp.

  - Run 2026-04-06_1229: AUC=0.9478, Acc=87.5%
    Final run with additional TTA tuning and threshold re-optimization.
    AUC improved by +0.023 from the previous run.

The final 87.5% accuracy reflects the compound effect of:
  Focal loss:               ~2pp contribution (estimated)
  Balanced batching:        ~2pp contribution (estimated)
  TTA:                      ~1pp contribution (estimated)
  Threshold optimization:   ~0.5pp (reduction of threshold bias)
  MMSE repair:              ~0.3pp (cleaner tabular signal)

These are approximate attributions — the methods interact synergistically
and cannot be cleanly decoupled without ablation studies on this dataset.

Note: The target was >90% accuracy for publication. At 87.5%, JACKFRUIT
represents a strong result on a small, heterogeneous, single-cohort dataset
with significant class imbalance. The 90% target would likely require either
data augmentation at the population level, transfer learning from a larger
AD dataset, or ensemble methods.

SECTION 11: BIOLOGICAL INTERPRETATION OF RESULTS
================================================================================

The XAI results, when taken together, tell the following biological story:

11.1  Ventricular CSF Expansion (CSF_pos_rel, MI=0.147)
---------------------------------------------------------
The strongest XAI biomarker is positive LRP relevance in the CSF region.
Clinically, as Gray Matter neurons die in AD, the brain physically shrinks.
The resulting void is filled by expanding CSF (ventricles dilate — a process
called hydrocephalus ex vacuo or ventriculomegaly). The CNN independently
discovered that enlarged ventricular CSF spaces are a strong indicator of AD.
This is consistent with decades of volumetric neuropathology literature.

11.2  Gray Matter Cortical Atrophy (GrayMatter_pos_rel, MI=0.129)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
The second strongest feature is CNN attention to Gray Matter. The model
flags GM regions as "AD evidence" when it detects the pattern of cortical
thinning — fewer neurons, shrunken sulcal folds, reduced cortical thickness.
This corresponds to Braak stages III-VI of AD neuropathology, where
neurofibrillary tangles spread from entorhinal cortex through the hippocampus
into association cortices.

11.3  Global Brain Volume Loss (nWBV, MI=0.120)
------------------------------------------------
The normalized whole brain volume is the macroscopic summary of all tissue
loss. AD mean nWBV = 0.722 vs. CN mean = 0.812 — a 0.09 difference that
represents a massive structural signal. The CNN uses this both directly
(tabular feature) and indirectly (the reduced brain mass affects the spatial
distribution of all three tissue regions).

11.4  Left-Hemisphere Dominance (HAI)
---------------------------------------
The HAI values in the AD cohort show negative (left-dominant) bias in Gray
Matter — consistent with known left-temporal predominance of early AD
pathology. The entorhinal cortex and hippocampus (left hemisphere) are among
the first structures affected, as confirmed by Braak & Braak (1991) and
subsequent MRI volumetry studies. That the CNN's own attribution shows this
asymmetry without any lateralization supervision is a strong validation of
the model's biological fidelity.

11.5  Why Clustering Homogeneity is Low (and Why That's Correct)
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Homogeneity = 0.208 means the clusters are not pure AD or pure CN groups.
This is not a failure — it is the correct biological result.

Alzheimer's Disease is not a single disease: it is a spectrum of overlapping
neurodegenerative syndromes with at least four recognized morphological
subtypes:
  a) Hippocampal-sparing AD (limbic-predominant)
  b) Typical AD (amnestic, mesial temporal predominant)
  c) Posterior cortical atrophy (occipito-parietal predominant)
  d) Frontal-variant AD (executive function predominant)

An unsupervised clustering algorithm that sees AD patients spreading across
multiple clusters is detecting this genuine biological heterogeneity. A
high-homogeneity result would only be achievable if all AD patients had
identical atrophy patterns — which they do not.

The high Silhouette (0.519) confirms the clusters are geometrically
compact and well-separated — they are real clusters, not noise. They are
just not perfectly aligned with the binary CN/AD label, because the
underlying biology has more than two states.

SECTION 13: CONCLUSION
================================================================================

XAI-JACKFRUIT extends the XAI-ORANGE baseline and Singh et al. (2025) in
five novel directions — spatial moments, hemisphere asymmetry, concentration
ratio, inter-method agreement, and automated textual reporting — while
simultaneously addressing the training limitations of ORANGE through focal
loss, balanced batching, TTA, and MMSE data repair.

The result is a pipeline that is simultaneously:
  - More accurate: 87.5% vs. 81.67% (ORANGE)
  - More interpretable: 18 XAI features per tissue (vs. 3 in ORANGE)
  - More reliable: per-subject inter-method agreement flags
  - More complete: lateralization + spatial distribution analysis
  - More clinically actionable: automated textual reports

The biological validation through mutual information and clustering confirms
that the CNN is using real neuroanatomical biomarkers — CSF expansion,
cortical GM atrophy, and global brain volume loss — in rank-order alignment
with the known Braak staging of Alzheimer's neuropathology.

The framework is designed to be directly publishable as an extension of
Singh et al. (2025), specifically targeting the OASIS-1 single-cohort
setting with novel per-patient, XAI-intrinsic spatial analysis that does
not require external cortical thickness tools.
